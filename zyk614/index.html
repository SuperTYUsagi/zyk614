Typhoon is one kind of tropicl cyclones, which is formed in northwestern Pacific with wind speeds of 17.2 meters per second or more.

Typhoons begin as tropical disturbances in warm ocean waters with surface temperatures of at least 26.5 degrees Celsius. Those low-pressure systems are fed by energy from warm seas.

A storm with wind speeds of 17.1 meters per second or less is classified as a tropical depression. It becomes a tropical storm—and is given a name, according to conventions determined by the World Meteorological Organization—when its sustained wind speeds top 17.2 meters per second.

Typhoons are enormous heat engines that deliver energy on a staggering scale. They draw heat from warm, moist ocean air and release it through condensation of water vapor in thunderstorms.

Typhoons spin around a low-pressure center known as the eye. Sinking air makes this 32- to 64-kilometer-wide area notoriously calm. But the eye is surrounded by a circular “eye wall” that contains the storm’s strongest winds and rain.
